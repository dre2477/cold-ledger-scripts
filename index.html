Series Overview

Genre: Corporate Psychological Thriller  |  Tone: Succession meets House of Cards — rooted entirely in Nigerian business culture, class anxiety, and the terrifying intimacy of people who built something together.

Central Question
When you are the most qualified person in the room, does that give you the right to take what belongs to someone else?

Character Profiles
Tunde Adebayo
Founder & Chairman — 52
6'2". Built an empire from a secondhand briefcase. Moves like a man who learned long ago that rushing is a signal of weakness. His eyes hold a question no one wants to answer.
Fatal flaw: Cannot accept being wrong. Would rather burn the building than admit the foundation cracked.
Chika Okafor
CFO — 44
Elegant, precise. Dresses to disappear until the moment she doesn't. Watched her father's wealth destroy her family at fifteen. Made herself the most dangerous kind of person — one who believes their intelligence makes them morally correct.
Fatal flaw: Cannot distinguish between protecting something and owning it.
Emeka Adebayo
Tunde's Son — 28
The son who was born into the answer and is still looking for the question. Both Tunde and Chika want his loyalty. Neither is entirely honest with him. The most human character in the series.
His role: The pivot point. The moral mirror.
Yetunde Adebayo
Tunde's Wife — 50
Former Zenith Bank executive. Saw everything. Spoke too late. Everyone underestimates her. It is the smartest thing she ever arranged.
Fatal flaw: Waited too long to demand to be heard.
Victor Nwosu
External Counsel — 39
No ideology. No loyalty except to outcome. He serves whoever holds the pen. The most honest character in the series — honest about exactly what he is.
He was working a third side the entire time.
Stella Okafor
Journalist — 42
Chika's younger sister. Same wound, different response. Where Chika built walls, Stella built windows. She does not yet know her sister is becoming the thing they both feared.
The witness. The truth-teller. The reckoning.
Season One — 2004–2012
Episode 1
"Secondhand Briefcase"
2004. Lagos. Before the empire.
Voiceover — Chika, Present Day
"People ask me when it began. The plan. The decision. They imagine I woke up one morning with a knife in my hand. But that is not how these things happen. They happen the way all slow disasters happen — gradually, and then all at once. The way a marriage dies. The way a river floods. The way a man you once respected becomes the reason you cannot sleep."
Image Prompt for Gemini — Scene 1.1
Cinematic wide shot, golden hour, Apapa Lagos waterfront 2004. Corrugated iron rooftops stretching toward distant port cranes silhouetted against an orange-pink sky. Yellow danfos, hawkers, smoke from roadside suya grills. Painterly, slightly hazy light. Film grain texture. Color palette: burnt orange, rust red, pale gold, deep shadow blue. Mood: hunger and possibility.
Camera Direction
WIDE ESTABLISHING SHOT — drone descending slowly through Lagos morning smog, tilting down toward a single figure on a street below. Then LOW ANGLE TRACKING SHOT — camera moves at street level alongside Tunde's feet, then rises slowly to frame his face.
Dialogue — Scene 1.2: The Reception
Receptionist
Yes?
Young Tunde (without breaking stride)
I have a nine o'clock with Mr. Fashola.
Receptionist (checks book)
I don't have your name here.
Young Tunde (pause — looks at her directly)
Then Mr. Fashola will have two conversations with me today. The one we scheduled — and the one about why his receptionist turned away the man who just secured him twelve thousand barrels.
(Beat. She picks up the phone.)
Voiceover — Tunde, Present Day
"My father left on a Tuesday. I remember because it was the day before he was supposed to sign my school fees form. I signed it myself — traced his signature from an old document. That was the day I learned that a signature is not a person. It is a performance of certainty. And certainty is something you can teach yourself."
Scene 1.10
The First Conversation
2009 — Tunde's Office, Victoria Island
Image Prompt for Gemini
Interior, Tunde's office, top floor, morning light. Large clean desk — almost empty, deliberate power statement. Young Chika, 27, sits in a chair, straight-backed, document folder on her lap, expression composed. Tunde, 35, stands by the floor-to-ceiling window, Lagos spread behind him. Warm morning light. Long shadows. Mood: the moment two minds recognize each other. Color palette: warm mahogany tones, the blue-silver of Lagos outside.
Camera Direction
WIDE SHOT holding both figures — Chika in foreground, Tunde across the room by the window. The space between them is alive. Then ALTERNATING CLOSE-UPS — Chika's hands on the folder, steady; Tunde's eyes, reading her.
Dialogue — The First Conversation
Tunde (by the window, not turning yet)
You've been here how long?
Chika
Twenty-two hours.
Tunde (turns)
And in those twenty-two hours you found something four senior analysts missed for three months.
Chika
They didn't miss it. They saw it. They just didn't know if they were supposed to say it.
Tunde
What's the difference?
Chika
Permission. They were waiting for permission. I wasn't hired to wait for permission. I was hired to read numbers. Numbers don't give permission.
Tunde
What do numbers give?
Chika (a beat)
Truth. Even when the person reading them doesn't want it.
Voiceover — Chika, Closing Episode 1
"People study the fall. They want to know when a great man breaks — what was the moment, what was the word, what was the choice. But the fall is not where the story begins. The story begins in the morning meetings. In the work sessions. In the first time someone sees you and says — you are more than what this room says you are. That is where it begins. In the gift. Not in the taking of it back."
Season Three — 2019–2022
Episode 4 — The Scene the Series Was Built For
"Face to Face"
48 Hours Before the Vote
Image Prompt for Gemini
Tunde's private conference room, Adebayo Group, Lagos, evening. Two people facing each other across a table cleared of everything except a single folder between them. TUNDE standing — he has been standing when she arrived, a deliberate choice. CHIKA seated — also a deliberate choice. Both fully composed. Glass walls on two sides, the Lagos skyline beyond. People passing outside — oblivious. The privacy of a glass room where nobody can hear. Overhead track lighting, cold and even. Mood: two extraordinary people at the absolute limit of what they built together. Color palette: cool white track light, gray glass walls, black suits, blue-silver Lagos evening, single white folder on the table.
Camera Direction
OPENS on the folder on the table. Two hands on opposite sides of the table. Then SLOW RISE to both faces. Both still. Both watching the other. Then — a long hold on this composition. Two people. One room. The empire between them.
Dialogue — The Confrontation (Full Scene)
(He speaks first. He always speaks first.)
Tunde
Close the door.
(She already closed it. She says nothing.)
Tunde
When.
(It is not a question.)
Tunde
When did you decide to take this company from me?
Chika
I decided to restructure the governance of this company when the chairman began making two-hundred-and-sixty-billion naira decisions that his CFO found out about through press releases.
Tunde (quieter — and this is worse)
I trusted you with everything. You were in my home. You were at my family's table. You were the person I brought to London when I wanted the room to see the best version of what this company was.
Tunde
Why?
Chika (the first unguarded moment)
Because you were going to destroy it.
Chika (standing — matching him)
Your CFO was trying to save the company from a chairman who has been replacing risk management with ego!
(The room holds that for a moment. Loud. Finally loud.)
Tunde (very quietly)
You fixed it — therefore you own it. Is that the logic?
Chika (the most honest thing she has said in the series)
I don't know. I don't know if that's the logic. I know that every time I fixed something, I was less sure that the thing I was protecting — was being protected. I know that I looked at this company and I saw what it could be — and I—
Tunde (very quietly)
You wanted it.
(The silence is full.)
Chika (barely audible)
Yes.
Tunde
Get out of my building, Chika. The vote is in forty-eight hours. We will not speak again until we speak in that boardroom.
Voiceover — Tunde
"She said yes. After everything — she said yes. And I want to tell you that in that moment I hated her. I want to tell you that because it would be cleaner. But the honest answer is that in that moment I understood her. I understood exactly who she was and why she had done what she'd done. And the understanding was more painful than the betrayal. Because understanding someone who has hurt you means you cannot even have your anger clean."
Episode 7
"Six to One"
The Vote That Tears Everything Open
Dialogue — The Vote
Tunde (calmly interrupting)
Before the vote — I have a statement for the record.
Tunde
I built this company with thirty thousand naira, two relationships, and a secondhand briefcase. I have built it over twenty-two years into a group with four point seven billion dollars in assets, seven hundred and forty employees, and operations in six sectors.
Tunde
What I will not accept is the suggestion that gratitude — or even indebtedness — is the same as surrender. I did not build this company to hand it to someone who built a plan inside my house while sitting at my table. If there are members of this board who believe the governance of this company requires the mechanism being put forward today — I invite them to say so to my face. Not through proxy agreements. Not through secondary counsel. To my face.
(Six hands rise. Then:)
Board Secretary
Those opposed—
Tunde (raising his hand — his own vote, against the motion)
One.
Board Secretary
The motion carries — six votes in favor, one opposed, five abstentions. Special Resolution 4 is passed.
Voiceover — Chika
"I won. Six to one. That is the number that history will record. And in the moment the Secretary announced it — the thing I expected to feel was absent. I expected clarity. The clarity of a completed mission. What I felt instead was a specific, sharp, irreversible knowledge: the man at the other end of that table would spend the rest of his life as the man who was voted out of his own company. And that his one vote would be the most important vote of his life. Not because it changed the result. But because it was the last act of a man who refused to surrender. I had won everything. And in winning, I had become the thing I grew up afraid of."
Season Five — Series Finale
Episode 8 — Series Finale
"The Last Meeting"
A rooftop. Lagos. The only honest conversation they ever had.
Image Prompt for Gemini — Final Scene
Rooftop restaurant terrace, Lagos Island, evening. The city spread below — the lagoon, island lights showing in blue-going-gold dusk. A single table. Two chairs. TUNDE, 52, in dark agbada — formal wear of an elder, chosen deliberately. CHIKA, 44, in a simple dress — not the armor of a suit. Both older than at the series start. Not broken. Weathered. Behind them: Lagos at dusk, beautiful and indifferent. Mood: two people who will never be fully at peace but can be, for one hour, fully honest. Color palette: Lagos dusk gold and blue, the worn warmth of the terrace, two people in the last light.
Camera Direction — Final Scene
WIDE SHOT — the terrace, the city, two small figures at the table. Then SLOWLY MOVING IN over the course of the scene — until the final shot holds both faces, close, the city behind them going dark. TIGHTEST shot of the series — EXTREME CLOSE-UP on Chika's face. The city lights reflected in her eyes. Every year of the series in that face. Then SLOW PULL BACK — wider, wider — until she is small again and the city is enormous around her.
Dialogue — The Final Scene
Chika
I was wrong.
(He is very still.)
Chika
Not about the governance issues. Not about the risk assessment. Those were real and I would document them the same way today. But the decision to — to use what I had access to, to build around you instead of with you—
Tunde (quietly)
Was what your father did.
(The air goes very still.)
Tunde
I made it possible. I need to say that. I dismissed the warnings — yours, Yetunde's, my own instincts. I chose comfort over accuracy every time the numbers told me something difficult. You were not wrong about that. I was building on arrogance by the end and I knew it. I chose not to know it.
Tunde
You are the most brilliant person I have ever worked with. That is a fact and it will remain a fact regardless of what the courts decide in March.
Chika (barely above a whisper)
Thank you.
Tunde (standing)
See you in March.
(He goes. She stays. The city fully dark now.)
Series Final Voiceover — Chika
"People will write about this as a story about power. About what happens when ambition runs ahead of ethics. They will use our names as cautionary tales. Fine. Let them.

What they will miss is that it was also a story about two people who spoke the same language and forgot, somewhere in the middle of all that building, that speaking the same language is not the same as having the same heart.

Tunde built an empire. I tried to inherit it without being given it. We were both wrong in the ways that brilliant, wounded people are wrong — completely, and with total conviction.

But in the column labeled — things I understand about what it means to be human — the balance is, I think, finally, coming right."
Final Image Prompt for Gemini
Lagos aerial shot, present day — the city at blue-hour before dawn. Wide, sweeping, the island and the mainland and the water between them. The lights of Adebayo Group visible — one lit building among thousands. No names. No people. Just the city, going on. Above it all: the sky just beginning to lighten at the edge of the world. Color palette: deep blue, the warm orange of city light, the first pale gold of dawn on the horizon's edge. Mood: the end of a particular story. The continuation of all the others.
