# COLD LEDGER — Complete Series Script (All 5 Seasons)

**Tagline:** "Every empire is built on someone else's silence."

---

## SERIES OVERVIEW

**Genre:** Corporate Psychological Thriller | **Setting:** Lagos, Nigeria 2004–2026

---

## CHARACTER PROFILES

### TUNDE ADEBAYO — Chairman & Founder
- 6'2", commanding, silver-templed. Built empire from a secondhand briefcase.
- Psychological wound: Father abandoned him at age 9. Every contract is proof of permanence.
- Fatal flaw: Cannot accept being wrong. Would rather burn the building.
- Speech: Slow, deliberate. Silences that make people confess.

### CHIKA OKAFOR — CFO
- Elegant, precise. Dresses to disappear until she doesn't want to.
- Psychological wound: Father's wealth destroyed her family. Mother lost everything in court at age 15.
- Fatal flaw: Cannot distinguish protecting something from owning it.
- Speech: Precise. Uses questions as scalpels.

### EMEKA ADEBAYO — Tunde's son, 28
- Born into the answer, still searching for the question.
- The pivot point. The most human character. Genuinely doesn't know which side is right.

### YETUNDE ADEBAYO — Tunde's wife, 50
- Former Zenith Bank executive. Saw everything. Spoke too late.
- Everyone underestimates her. It is the smartest thing she ever arranged.

### BABA ROTIMI — Board Member, 68
- Tunde's first business partner. Remembers who Tunde was before the empire.
- The moral compass with no power to enforce it.

### STELLA OKAFOR — Journalist, 42
- Chika's younger sister. Same wound, different response.
- Where Chika built walls, Stella built windows.

### VICTOR NWOSU — External Counsel, 39
- No loyalty except to outcome. Uses everyone.
- The most honest character — honest about what he is.

### HARUNA MUSA — COO, 48
- Tunde's old guard. Believes in the original Tunde like scripture.

**[DIALOGUE — Scene 1.10: The First Conversation]**

**TUNDE:** *(window, not turning yet)* You've been here how long?

**CHIKA:** Twenty-two hours.

**TUNDE:** *(turns)* And in those twenty-two hours you found something four senior analysts missed for three months.

**CHIKA:** They didn't miss it. They saw it. They just didn't know if they were supposed to say it.

**TUNDE:** What's the difference?

**CHIKA:** Permission. They were waiting for permission. I wasn't hired to wait for permission. I was hired to read numbers. Numbers don't give permission.

*(He almost smiles.)*

**TUNDE:** What do numbers give?

**CHIKA:** Truth. Even when the person reading them doesn't want it.

*(Long pause. He sits. Studies her.)*

**TUNDE:** Are you telling me something?

**CHIKA:** I'm telling you the numbers are telling something. I'm just the messenger.

**CHIKA:** I thought it might save time.

**TUNDE:** This is good work.

**CHIKA:** Thank you.

**TUNDE:** You're not a junior analyst.

**TUNDE:** Senior Analyst. Effective today.

## EPISODE 3: "WHAT THE NUMBERS KNOW" — 2012-2014

**[VOICEOVER — Chika, Cold Open]**
"A balance sheet is a confession. Most people do not know how to read a confession. They see the numbers. I see what the numbers are hiding. And in 2013, the numbers at Adebayo Group began hiding something very large."

---

**[IMAGE PROMPT FOR GEMINI — Scene 3.1: Late Night Discovery]**
*CFO's office, Adebayo Group, 11 PM. Building almost empty. CHIKA at desk, multiple screens, financial models running. Single lamp. Jacket removed. Reading glasses. Multiple printouts marked with red pen. Her expression: someone who has found what they hoped not to find. Lagos at night through window. Color palette: dark blue night, warm amber lamp, red marks on white paper.*

**[CAMERA: EXTREME CLOSE-UP — red pen circling a figure. PULL BACK to reveal the full desk's complexity. CLOSE-UP on her face: she removes glasses, rubs her eyes. Puts them back on. Looks at screen as if hoping numbers changed.]**

---

**[DIALOGUE — Scene 3.2: The 11:23 PM Call]**

**VICTOR:** CFO.

**CHIKA:** Victor. The Abuja corridor acquisition — what's the full structure?

**VICTOR:** *(pause — too short for coincidence, too long for comfort)* Why?

**CHIKA:** I can see the acquisition cost but not the full collateral package. The covenant section references a supplementary agreement I don't have.

**VICTOR:** That agreement is held at a different level.

**CHIKA:** What level, Victor?

**VICTOR:** Personal guarantees. Tunde's personal asset portfolio is in the collateral mix. He didn't want it on the corporate balance sheet.

*(Silence. The air changes.)*

**CHIKA:** He's personally guaranteeing corporate debt. How much?

**VICTOR:** *(quietly)* Seven-point-four billion naira. Across three agreements.

*(She writes the number. In capitals. With a period after it.)*

**CHIKA:** Send me everything. Tonight. I will tell Tunde myself.

---

**[VOICEOVER — Chika]**
"Seven-point-four billion naira. That was the first secret. Not the last — but the first. And in that moment, sitting in my office at eleven-twenty at night, I was still loyal. I want that written down. I was still loyal."

---

**[IMAGE PROMPT FOR GEMINI — Scene 3.3: Midnight at Tunde's Home]**
*Tunde Adebayo's home office, Ikoyi Lagos, midnight. Large private study — bookshelves, framed contracts, family photograph. TUNDE in house robe over shirt. CHIKA still in work clothes, folder on desk between them. The desk lamp. Through study window: tropical garden, still at night. Mood: the moment a private secret enters shared space. Color palette: warm amber study light, deep tropical darkness outside.*

---

**[DIALOGUE — Scene 3.4: Midnight Warning]**

**TUNDE:** You drove here at midnight.

**CHIKA:** You have personal guarantees on seven-point-four billion naira of corporate debt that are not reflected in the financial statements I have been signing.

*(His hands flat on the documents.)*

**CHIKA:** As CFO, I sign those statements. I am personally and legally accountable. This is not a conversation I could have in the morning.

**TUNDE:** The guarantees are legal.

**CHIKA:** I didn't say they weren't legal. I said they're not disclosed. The difference is our external auditors and every institutional investor who reviewed those statements in good faith.

*(He stands. Goes to window. Back to her.)*

**TUNDE:** I needed the financing. The lender required the personal guarantee. If I'd put it on the corporate balance sheet, the Meridian covenants would have triggered. You know that.

**CHIKA:** I know. That's why I'm here instead of writing a formal report. I'm here because this cannot stay where it is. We need to restructure. Bring it into the light.

**TUNDE:** *(turning)* Is there a way?

**CHIKA:** There might be. I need six weeks. And complete access to all supplementary agreements.

*(A beat that lasts longer than it should.)*

**TUNDE:** All right.

*(She picks up her folder. Stands.)*

**CHIKA:** Tunde. How many more are there?

*(He holds her gaze. Not a lie exactly. Not a full truth.)*

**TUNDE:** The six weeks starts tomorrow.

*(She nods. She goes. He stands at the window of his garden. Alone with the first full weight of what he has built — and what it is costing.)*

---

**[DIALOGUE — Scene 3.6: Six Weeks Later. The Restructuring.]**

**TUNDE:** This works.

**CHIKA:** It works. But it requires full board disclosure by Q2.

**TUNDE:** Q2 is our expansion announcement. The press conference—

**CHIKA:** We can sequence it. Board disclosure before the announcement. The announcement absorbs the news cycle.

**CHIKA:** Tunde. We cannot have another midnight discovery.

*(He looks at her. Something — not quite gratitude. Something that looks almost like relief — and almost like the beginning of resentment.)*

**TUNDE:** Make it happen.

*(As she turns to go:)*

**TUNDE:** Chika. Thank you.

*(She pauses at the door. Doesn't turn.)*

**CHIKA:** Don't give me a reason to come to your house at midnight again.

---

**[VOICEOVER — Tunde, closing Episode 3]**
"She saved me in 2013. I knew it then. I know it now. I told her thank you, and she told me not to give her reason to do it again. And what I heard — underneath those words — was: next time, I may not come. And I was afraid, for the first time in my adult life, of losing someone's presence. Not their service. Their presence. That fear was the beginning of my real problem. Because the things we are afraid of losing, we often push away. Harder and harder, until the fear becomes the prophecy."

## EPISODE 4: "WHAT LOYALTY LOOKS LIKE" — 2014

**[IMAGE PROMPT FOR GEMINI — The Argument]**
*TUNDE's office, evening, after hours. CHIKA standing — hasn't sat down. Jacket still on. Printed press release on desk between them — pipeline consortium announcement. Her body language: contained fury. TUNDE seated — signal of authority — but his eyes know what's coming. Evening gold going orange through window. Mood: the first real fracture.*

**[CAMERA: STATIC WIDE — both figures. Press release between them. TIGHT ON Chika's hands: very still. Controlled fury is still fury.]**

---

**[DIALOGUE — Scene 4.1: The Argument]**

**CHIKA:** *(no raised voice)* I found out from a press release, Tunde.

**TUNDE:** The announcement timing was moved up. There was a—

**CHIKA:** I found out from a press release. That a two-hundred-and-sixty-billion-naira commitment had been made. With partners I have not risk-reviewed. In a region with three active conflict advisories.

*(He is quiet.)*

**CHIKA:** When was this decided?

**TUNDE:** Four weeks ago. In Abuja.

**CHIKA:** *(computing)* Who in the building knew?

*(A beat that says everything.)*

**CHIKA:** Haruna knew.

*(He says nothing. She walks to the window. Three steps away from him.)*

**CHIKA:** You brought it to the COO and not the CFO. On a financial commitment of this scale.

**TUNDE:** *(standing now)* I know what you sign.

*(The room changes temperature.)*

**CHIKA:** *(quieter — which is more dangerous)* Do you? Because if you know what I sign — and you made this decision without me — then we have a different kind of problem.

**TUNDE:** I am the chairman of this company. The final decision on strategic investments—

**CHIKA:** Yes. You are. And I am the CFO. And those two roles only work if the information between them moves in both directions. What you've done — three times in two years — is make decisions at a scale I am then responsible for disclosing, without being part of the decision.

**TUNDE:** Find a way to make it work.

*(Something shifts in her face — very small, very controlled, very permanent.)*

**CHIKA:** *(quietly)* All right. *(at the door)* For the record — I can make it work. I have been making it work. What I can't do is protect the company from decisions I'm not part of. That's the chairman's job.

*(She leaves.)*

**[CAMERA: STATIC on Tunde alone. He slowly sits. Looks at the press release. The face of a man who knows he was wrong and cannot say so.]**

---

**[VOICEOVER — Chika, closing Episode 4]**
"That night I drove home and I sat in my car in the garage for forty-five minutes. And I asked myself: is this his company, or is it mine? Not in the legal sense. In the sense that matters. Who understands it? Who protects it? Who should be making these decisions? And the terrifying thing — the answer I gave myself was not: it's his company, and I serve him. The answer I gave was: I should be making these decisions. And once you give yourself that answer — once — the loyalty starts to become something else. I did not plan what came after. But I gave myself permission for it. That night. In the garage. Sitting in the dark."

## SERIES FINAL VOICEOVER — Chika

"People will write about this as a story about power. About corporate governance. About what happens when ambition runs ahead of ethics. They will put it in business school curricula — what not to do. They will use our names as cautionary tales. Fine. Let them.

Tunde built an empire. I tried to inherit it without being given it. We were both wrong in the ways that brilliant, wounded people are wrong — completely, and with total conviction.

The company survived. It is smaller now, and more careful, and governed by people who understood — only after watching us — what governance actually means.

Tunde is non-executive chairman. He comes in on Tuesdays. He sits in meetings and asks the questions that nobody else asks, slowly, in the silence that still makes people sweat. He has not lost that.

I consult. Independent. I work with companies that have the same problems we had — the same concentration of decision-making, the same conflation of instinct with authority, the same blind spots dressed as confidence. I see them coming from a long way off now. I know exactly how they develop.

My sister asked me once — after everything — whether I regretted it.

I told her what I always tell people when they ask the wrong question: I don't do regret. Regret is retrospective. I do accounting. And the accounting on my life shows a significant deficit in the column labeled — things I built and kept.

But in the column labeled — things I understand about what it means to be human — the balance is, I think, finally, coming right."
